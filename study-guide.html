Diversity & Immigration

  • The UK today is more ethnically and religiously diverse than 100 years ago.
  • Post-war immigration means nearly 10% of the population has a parent or grandparent born outside the UK.
  • The UK is described as a multinational and multiracial society with a rich and varied culture.
  • Most people live in towns and cities, but much of Britain is still countryside.

Religion in the UK

The UK is a Christian country but also has a wide variety of other religions. There is no legal requirement to follow any faith.

Christianity — The Established Churches

ChurchCountryHead
Church of England (Anglican)EnglandThe King (Supreme Governor)
Church of Scotland (Presbyterian)ScotlandNo royal head (independent)
Roman Catholic ChurchAll UKThe Pope
Church in WalesWalesArchbishop of Wales
Methodist ChurchAll UK—
⚠️ The Church of Scotland is Presbyterian — the monarch is NOT its head. This is a common exam trick. In England, the King IS the Supreme Governor of the Church of England.

Other Religions

☪️ Islam

  • About 3% of the UK population
  • Largest non-Christian faith
  • Key festivals: Eid ul-Fitr (end of Ramadan), Eid ul-Adha

🕉️ Hinduism

  • About 1% of population
  • Key festival: Diwali (Festival of Lights)
  • Also celebrates Holi

✡️ Judaism

  • Long history in UK
  • Key festival: Hannukah, Yom Kippur, Passover
  • Shabbat observed weekly

🪬 Sikhism

  • Strong presence especially in Punjab-heritage communities
  • Festival: Vaisakhi (New Year / harvest)

☸️ Buddhism

  • Growing community in UK
  • Festival: Vesak (Buddha's birthday)

🚫 No religion

  • A significant and growing portion of the population identifies as non-religious

Other Religious Festivals

🕎 Hannukah Jewish

8-day Jewish festival of lights; typically November/December; commemorates rededication of the Temple in Jerusalem.

🪔 Diwali Hindu / Sikh

Festival of Lights; October/November; celebrated by Hindus, Sikhs and Jains; lamps lit, fireworks, sweets exchanged.

🌙 Eid ul-Fitr Muslim

End of Ramadan fasting month; prayers, feasting, gifts to children; date varies by Islamic calendar.

🐂 Eid ul-Adha Muslim

Festival of Sacrifice; commemorates Ibrahim's willingness to sacrifice his son; prayers and sharing of food.

🌼 Vaisakhi Sikh

14 April; Sikh New Year and harvest festival; also marks founding of the Khalsa in 1699; celebrated with processions.

🔥 Holi Hindu

Spring festival of colour; celebrated widely in Hindu communities in UK; throwing coloured powder.

Remembrance Sunday — Key Details

  • Held on the 2nd Sunday in November each year.
  • Commemorates those who died in WWI, WWII and other conflicts.
  • A 2-minute silence is observed at 11:00am on 11 November (marking the end of WWI in 1918).
  • People wear red poppies as a symbol of remembrance, inspired by Flanders fields in Belgium.
  • Services held at the Cenotaph in Whitehall, London.

Famous Sporting Events

🎾 Wimbledon

World's oldest tennis Grand Slam. Held at the All England Club since 1877. Strawberries and cream are a tradition.

🏇 The Grand National

Famous horse race at Aintree racecourse, Liverpool. One of the most watched TV events in UK.

🚣 Oxford–Cambridge Boat Race

Annual rowing race on the River Thames. First held in 1829.

⛳ The Open Championship

One of golf's four Grand Slams; rotates around UK courses; often held at St Andrews.

🏏 The Ashes

Cricket series between England and Australia played since 1882. Highly competitive and historic.

🏆 FA Cup

World's oldest football competition, started in 1872. Open to all English football clubs.

Olympics & Paralympics

  • The UK has hosted the Olympics three times: 1908, 1948, and 2012 (London).
  • The 2012 London Olympics and Paralympics were considered hugely successful.
  • The Paralympics began in 1948 at Stoke Mandeville Hospital by Sir Ludwig Guttmann, a German-born doctor.
  • British athletes have performed exceptionally well in cycling, rowing, sailing, and athletics at modern Olympics.
  • Notable British Olympians: Sir Chris Hoy (cycling), Bradley Wiggins (cycling/Tour de France), Jessica Ennis-Hill (heptathlon), Mo Farah (long distance running), Andy Murray (tennis — won 2012 Olympic gold).
⚠️ Know that Sir Roger Bannister was the first person to run a mile in under 4 minutes (1954). Sir Steve Redgrave won gold medals at 5 consecutive Olympics in rowing.

Arts & Culture

Literature — Key Authors

William Shakespeare
1564–1616
Greatest playwright in English; wrote 37 plays & 154 sonnets. Globe Theatre, London. Famous works: Hamlet, Macbeth, A Midsummer Night's Dream.
Jane Austen
1775–1817
Novels about women in society. Pride and Prejudice, Sense and Sensibility, Emma. Featured on £10 note.
Charles Dickens
1812–1870
Novels depicting Victorian poverty. Oliver Twist, A Christmas Carol, Great Expectations, David Copperfield.
Robert Burns
1759–1796
National poet of Scotland. "Bard of Ayrshire." Auld Lang Syne. Burns Night celebrated 25 January.
Thomas Hardy
1840–1928
Author & poet; rural society. Far from the Madding Crowd, Jude the Obscure.
Sir Arthur Conan Doyle
1859–1930
Scottish doctor & writer. Creator of Sherlock Holmes — one of the first fictional detectives.
Evelyn Waugh
1903–1966
Satirical novels: Decline and Fall, Scoop, Brideshead Revisited.
Graham Greene
1904–1991
Novels exploring moral ambiguity. The Third Man, Brighton Rock.
Roald Dahl
1916–1990
Children's stories: Charlie and the Chocolate Factory, James and the Giant Peach, Matilda.

Poetry — Key Poets

Geoffrey Chaucer
c.1340–1400
The Canterbury Tales — one of first works in English language. "Father of English literature."
William Wordsworth
1770–1850
Romantic poet; Lake District. Daffodils ("I Wandered Lonely as a Cloud"). Co-wrote Lyrical Ballads with Coleridge.
Lord Byron
1788–1824
Romantic poet. Childe Harold's Pilgrimage. Famous for wild lifestyle.
Wilfred Owen
1893–1918
WWI war poetry. Dulce et Decorum Est — critique of glorifying war. Killed 1 week before Armistice.
Dylan Thomas
1914–1953
Welsh poet. Do Not Go Gentle into That Good Night. Radio play Under Milk Wood.
Sir Walter Scott
1771–1832
Scottish novelist & poet. Ivanhoe, Rob Roy. Hugely popular in 19th century.

Music

  • Henry Purcell (1659–1695) — Baroque composer; organist at Westminster Abbey.
  • George Frideric Handel (1685–1759) — German-born, became British citizen; composed Messiah, Water Music, Music for the Royal Fireworks.
  • The Beatles (1960s) — John Lennon, Paul McCartney, George Harrison, Ringo Starr; most successful band in popular music history; from Liverpool.
  • The Rolling Stones — alongside The Beatles, led the British Invasion of American music in 1960s.
  • Andrew Lloyd Webber — hugely successful composer of musicals; Cats, The Phantom of the Opera, Jesus Christ Superstar.
  • UK has strong tradition of choral music, orchestras, and classical music via BBC Proms (annual summer festival at Royal Albert Hall).
  • The BRIT Awards are the UK's equivalent of the Grammys.

Theatre & Film

  • The UK has a proud theatrical tradition. West End (London) is world-famous for musicals and theatre alongside Broadway.
  • Shakespeare's Globe Theatre was built in 1599 in Southwark, London.
  • Famous British directors: Alfred Hitchcock (the "Master of Suspense"; Psycho, The Birds), Sir Ridley Scott.
  • The BAFTA awards are the UK's film & television awards.
  • Notable British films: The Third Man, Brief Encounter, Lawrence of Arabia, Four Weddings and a Funeral, Chariots of Fire, The Full Monty.
  • Pinewood Studios near London is a major film production centre (James Bond films made there).
  • British actors who became international stars: Sir Laurence Olivier, Sir Alec Guinness, Dame Judi Dench, Sir Anthony Hopkins, Helen Mirren.

Architecture

Sir Christopher Wren 1632–1723

Designed St Paul's Cathedral and 50+ London churches after the Great Fire of 1666. Also designed the Royal Observatory at Greenwich.

Inigo Jones 1573–1652

Introduced Palladian architecture to England. Designed the Banqueting House in Whitehall.

Sir Norman Foster b. 1935

Contemporary architect. The Gherkin (30 St Mary Axe), the Millennium Bridge, new Wembley Stadium.

Dame Zaha Hadid 1950–2016

Iraqi-born British architect. First woman to win the Pritzker Prize. Aquatics Centre for 2012 Olympics.

Art & Fashion

  • National Gallery (Trafalgar Square, London) — houses 2,300+ paintings from the 13th–19th centuries; free entry.
  • Tate Modern (Bankside, London) — UK's gallery for modern and contemporary art; free entry.
  • Mary Quant — British fashion designer who invented the mini skirt in the 1960s; symbol of the "Swinging Sixties."
  • Alexander McQueen — world-renowned British fashion designer.
  • Turner Prize — prestigious annual award for British visual artists.
  • Chelsea Flower Show — world's most famous horticultural show, held in London each May by the Royal Horticultural Society (RHS).

Leisure

Leisure and recreation are important in British life. The UK has a wide variety of activities, many of which are deeply rooted in culture and history.

🌸 Gardening

One of the most popular pastimes. The UK has a strong gardening culture; famous for its parks and garden shows (Chelsea Flower Show).

🍺 Pubs & Social Life

Pubs (public houses) are central to British social life — meeting points for communities. Many have pub quizzes, sports screens and live music.

🏞️ Countryside

Thousands of public footpaths and trails. Walking, camping, fishing, and cycling are popular outdoor activities.

🎲 Gambling & Betting

Carefully regulated in the UK. Horse racing betting is traditional. Strict age limits apply (18+).

🍳 Cooking

Growing popularity; reflects multicultural society. British food culture has expanded enormously since the 1990s.

📺 Television & Reading

Television is very popular. The BBC is the world's oldest national broadcaster. Reading remains widespread — UK has a strong literary culture.

National Trust & Conservation

  • The National Trust (England, Wales, NI) and National Trust for Scotland protect historic buildings, coastlines, and countryside.
  • They are charities funded by membership fees and donations.
  • The UK has 15 national parks across England, Scotland, and Wales, protecting wildlife and landscapes.
  • Notable national parks: Lake District, Snowdonia, Dartmoor, Peak District, Cairngorms (Scotland's largest).

Museums & Galleries

🏛️ British Museum

London. Free entry. One of the world's greatest museums — houses the Rosetta Stone, Elgin Marbles and millions of artefacts from across human history.

🖼️ National Gallery

Trafalgar Square, London. Free. Houses over 2,300 European paintings from the 13th–19th century.

🎨 Tate Modern

South Bank, London. Free. UK's national gallery for international modern and contemporary art, inside a former power station.

🔭 Science Museum

South Kensington, London. Free. One of the world's leading science & technology museums.

🦕 Natural History Museum

South Kensington, London. Free. Famous for its dinosaur exhibits and the Hope, a blue whale skeleton.

🏺 Victoria & Albert Museum

South Kensington, London. Free. World's largest museum of art and design — ceramics, textiles, fashion, sculpture.

ℹ️ Most major national museums and galleries in the UK are free to enter. This is an important and distinctive feature of UK public life.

Cathedrals & Religious Sites

  • Westminster Abbey — London; site of coronations since 1066 and many royal weddings and funerals; burial place of kings and notable figures.
  • St Paul's Cathedral — London; designed by Sir Christopher Wren; venue for major national services including Princess Diana's funeral and Charles & Diana's wedding.
  • Canterbury Cathedral — Kent; seat of the Archbishop of Canterbury; UNESCO World Heritage Site; pilgrimage site since the Middle Ages (Chaucer's Canterbury Tales).
  • York Minster — York; one of the largest Gothic cathedrals in Northern Europe.
  • St Giles' Cathedral — Edinburgh; High Kirk of Edinburgh; seat of the Church of Scotland.
